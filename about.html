My Journey

Current Research

I'm currently a graduate student at the University of Colorado Boulder and an NSF Graduate Research Fellow. My research integrates field ecology, statistical modeling, and geospatial science to understand how microclimatic patterns influence species persistence. I use computational tools and R programming to create predictive models that bridge the gap between weather station measurements and the fine-scale thermal environments organisms actually experience.

At the intersection of climate science and population ecology, my work examines how talus slopes create thermal refugia for alpine species, and how these microhabitats may buffer against rising temperatures in our changing climate.

Beyond the Lab

Science must extend beyond academia. Throughout my career, I've been committed to conservation outreach and education. From protecting Leatherback turtle nests from poachers in Baja California to teaching high school students about pika ecology at Niwot Ridge, I believe in inspiring the next generation of environmental stewards.

I've coached undergraduate students in R programming, led field trips for high school students through CU Boulder's Science Discovery program, and conducted science outreach for children in underserved communities. These experiences remind me that conservation requires not just research, but also community engagement and knowledge sharing.

Philosophy

My mother always told me, "Education is expensive, but it is more expensive to be ignorant." These words continue to drive me. I believe that understanding the smallest details of how organisms interact with their environment can reveal broader patterns about ecosystem resilience and our shared future on this planet.

There is nothing more captivating than wildlife observation, and anything that brings joy in a stressful world must be treasured and protected. Through integrating technology, statistics, and simulation of biological systems, we can learn to conserve our wildlife and, in turn, better understand ourselves and our future as a species.
